Abstract

This document presents a speculative but dimensionally consistent version of the earlier UTEG idea. Rather than asserting that time is literally equal to E/c³ (which is dimensionally a mass-per-length), we introduce a temporal potential Φ(x,y) derived from the local energy density and a reference scale so that Φ carries the dimension of time. We then propose a simple, physically sensible evolution law linking spatial curvature of Φ to its temporal dynamics.

1. Introduction

Motivated by the intuition that energy structure shapes how processes unfold, we construct a phenomenological field — the temporal potential Φ — that maps local energy density to a time-scaled field. Φ is not a replacement for coordinate time t, nor is it meant to violate relativity; it is a scalar field that can modulate local rates (in a toy-model sense) and that can be visualized using gradients and Laplacians.

2. Fundamental Postulates

  1. Temporal-potential hypothesis: A local scalar Φ(x,t) exists, derived from energy density, with SI dimension of time.
  2. Coupling: Spatial curvature of Φ (∇²Φ) sources temporal evolution of Φ through a wave-like PDE with propagation speed ceff.
  3. Recover GR / QM limits: The model is strictly phenomenological; where applicable it reduces to standard results (E = mc², energy-time uncertainty) and respects dimensional analysis.

3. Core Equations (corrected)

3.1 Definition (temporal potential)

Φ(x,y) := E(x,y) / ( ρ₀ · c³ )

Here E is local energy density (J·m⁻² for a 2D energy texture, or J·m⁻³ for a volumetric density — be explicit in implementation); ρ₀ is a chosen reference mass density (kg·m⁻³) that fixes units so Φ has units of seconds. This scaling is introduced purely to make Φ time-dimensioned; physics conclusions should not hinge on the numerical value of ρ₀.

3.2 Spatial derivatives

∇Φ = (∇E) / (ρ₀ · c³)  ∇²Φ = (∇²E) / (ρ₀ · c³)

These are directly visualizable: gradient direction = tilt of Φ, laplacian = curvature (attention wells in the earlier language).

3.3 Evolution (toy PDE)

∂²Φ/∂t² = c_eff² · ∇²Φ + S(x,t)

This is a simple wave equation for Φ with a source term S(x,t) (e.g., external injections of energy) and adjustable propagation speed ceff. It ensures dimensional consistency and produces stable behavior for visualization and experimentation.

3.4 Useful identities (standard physics retained)

E = m c²  (energy–mass equivalence)
ΔE · Δt ≥ ħ/2  (energy–time uncertainty; Δt is a timescale associated with the energy fluctuation)

4. Lagrangian (sketch)

One may write a phenomenological action that couples Φ to geometry and matter. A minimal, schematic action is:

S = ∫ d⁴x √(-g) [ (R / 16πG) + L_matter + ½ (∂_μ Φ)(∂^μ Φ) - V(Φ) + L_int(Φ, T_μν) ]

Here L_int encodes how Φ couples to the stress–energy (Tμν) or to effective local rates. This is intentionally schematic — the goal is to provide a consistent starting point for simulations and dimensional checks.

5. Quantum remarks

The energy–time uncertainty relation remains the operative quantum guideline: a transient energy fluctuation ΔE is associated with a timescale Δt with ΔE·Δt ≳ ħ/2. If Φ acts as a local timescale modulator in a phenomenological model, measurable effects would enter via standard quantum observables (spectral lines, transition rates).

ΔE · Δt ≥ ħ/2

6. Predictions & tests

6.1 Dimensional checks

  • Confirm Φ has units of seconds in your implementation: [E] / ( [ρ₀]·[c]³ ) → s.
  • Replace any instance of E/c³ used as 'time' with Φ or remove the interpretation.

6.2 Laboratory thought experiments

  • Atomic clocks: test whether local engineered variations in energy density (e.g., intense fields) produce measurable rate shifts beyond GR predictions — unlikely in this toy model, but conceptually testable.
  • Spectroscopy: relate frequency shifts to local Φ variations in the model and compare to controlled experiments.

6.3 Simulation tests

  • Implement E(x,y) texture → compute Φ(x,y) with chosen ρ₀ → evolve Φ by the PDE; visualize ∇Φ and ∇²Φ.
  • Check stability for a range of c_eff and grid resolutions.

Appendix — Units & dictionary (corrected)

A.1 Why E/c³ is not time

[E] (J) = kg·m²·s⁻². [c³] = m³·s⁻³. Thus

[E/c³] = (kg·m²·s⁻²) / (m³·s⁻³) = kg·s·m⁻¹ = M·T·L⁻¹

E/c³ has dimensions mass×time/length, which is not seconds. It behaves like a linear mass density times time. This is why we divide by a reference density ρ₀ to obtain seconds.

A.2 Φ dictionary (SI)

Φ ≡ E / (ρ₀ c³) ⇒ [Φ] = s
∇Φ = (∇E) / (ρ₀ c³)  ∇²Φ = (∇²E) / (ρ₀ c³)
E = m c² (mass–energy equivalence)
λ_C = ħ / (m c) (compton wavelength)
t_C = ħ / (m c²) (characteristic quantum timescale)

A.3 Planck scales (standard)

t_P = √(ħ G / c⁵) ≈ 5.39×10⁻⁴⁴ s
ℓ_P = √(ħ G / c³) ≈ 1.62×10⁻³⁵ m

These standard Planck scales are unchanged and serve as a reminder that quantum gravity effects are at extremely small scales; introducing Φ does not alter these constants.

A.4 Operational checks

  • In code, choose ρ₀ to match your E units (e.g., if E is J·m⁻² for a 2D canvas, pick ρ₀ with units kg·m⁻² so division yields seconds).
  • When mapping Φ to audio rate or visual speed, document the conversion factor and its units explicitly.
  • Any formula that previously read t ≈ m / c should be removed or replaced by a dimensionally correct timescale (e.g., t_C = ħ/(m c²)).
